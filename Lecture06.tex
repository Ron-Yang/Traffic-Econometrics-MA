\frame{\frametitle{Basic terms for surveys}
%##############################################################

\bfdef{Statistical unit/subject}: Literally the subject of the
investigation. Often one person or one houshold, but can also be one city
\vspace{1em}

\pause \bfdef{Population}:
Generally, a set of statistical units. Must be delimited with respect to
\bi
\item \emph{space}, e.g., Germany, certain cities
\pause \item \emph{time}, e.g., a certain date, the year 2018, or 2003 till
  open-end
\pause \item \emph{attributes}, i.e., properties of the statistical unit. 
\pause If the unit is a
  person, possible delimitations are, e.g., 
\bi
\item age: everyone or only adult persons
\item gender
\item if the space delimitation is Germany: does the population
  include only German citizens or foreigners as well? What about
  Germans living abroad?
\ei
\ei
\vspace{1em}

\pause \bfdef{Samples}: Random or layered samples with or without clustering
drawn from a specified register/data basis
}

\frame{\frametitle{The question about the age and income}
%##############################################################

Tricky questions!

\bi
\item Asking for the birth year is more neutral and gives the
  same information as asking for the age
\pause \item Asking for age groups is less intrusive and often sufficient for
  the objective of the investigation \\[1em]
\pause \item Nobody wants to reveal his/her true income 
\item Nor do most people
  know it exactly. It also is conditional to after/before taxes,
  social security etc.\\[1em]
\pause \item \colAsk{Solution?}
\pause \bi 
\pause \item[(i)] \colAnswer{Ask relative to a reference  
(``the average income after
  taxes is 1\,300 \euro{} per month'')}
\pause \item[(ii)] \colAnswer{Use categories 
 (``much more, more, about the same, less,
  much less'') in a Likert scale}
\ei
\ei
}

\subsection{6.4 Degree of Subjectivity}

%##############################################################
\frame{\frametitle{6.4 Degree of Subjectivity}
%##############################################################
\fig{0.9\textwidth}{figsErh/DegreeSubjectivity.png}
\benum
\pause \item Objective/physical measurement, e.g., automated traffic counts
\pause \item \bfdef{Revealed choice} or \bfdef{revealed preference (SP)}: People are
  asked about some realized activity or choice, i.e., the
  chain/sequence of trips and 
  their purposes they did at a given day
\pause \item \bfdef{Stated choice} or \bfdef{stated preference (SP)}: People
  are asked about an \emph{hypothetical} situation and how they would
  act/decide if confronted with it
\eenum
\visible<4->{\footnotesize{(strictly speaking, 
revealed/stated preference gives a  ranking of the 
preferences of the available alternatives while revealed/stated choice
only gives the favourite. However, this is often confused in the
literature)}}
 

}

%###################################################
\frame{\frametitle{6.4.3 Stated Preference/Choice (SP)}
%###################################################
\bi
\item Not everything can be asked with SP surveys: 
\pause \bi
\item Prices in future may be different shifting the weights of
  some alternatives
\item Some destinations (e.g., a future market) may not yet exist
\item Other modes exist but very little people make use of it (yet),
  e.g., BEVs
\ei
\pause \item Only with SP surveys, one can ``go to the limits'' where people
  change their mind $\Rightarrow$ better information per previous
  respondent minute!
\ei
}

\frame{\frametitle{6.4.4 Choice-based conjoint analysis\\
1. Exhaustive enumeration}
%###################################################

\bi
\item Total travel time ped/bike $T_1$: 10, 15, 20, 25 und 30 minutes,\\[-2em]
\item Total PT travel time  $T_2$: 10, 15, 20, 25 und 30 minutes,\\[-2em]

\ei

\vspace{2em}

  & {\small PT travel time $T_2$} & {\small PT ad-hoc costs $K_2$}

%###################################################
\frame{\frametitle{2. Orthogonal design}
%###################################################
\begin{tabular}{|r|c|c|c|} \hline
$i$ & {\small Ped/bike travel time $T_1$}
  & {\small PT travel time $T_2$} & {\small PT ad-hoc costs $K_2$}
   \\ \hline
2 & \unit[10]{min} & \unit[10]{min} & \unit[1.50]{\euro{}}\\
18 & \unit[10]{min} & \unit[30]{min} & \unit[1.50]{\euro{}}\\
84 & \unit[30]{min} & \unit[10]{min} &\unit[3.00]{\euro{}}\\
99 & \unit[30]{min} & \unit[30]{min} &\unit[3.00]{\euro{}}\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\vspace{1em}

\bi
\item No correlation (orthogonality) 
between the travel times of the two alternatives
\item Correlation allowed between attributes that are correlated in
  reality such as time and costs
  (here, $\text{Cov}(T_1,K_2)=0$ but $\text{Cov}(T_2,K_2)>0$)
\item Otherwise, dominant alternatives are nearly unavoidable in some
  choice sets
\ei

}
